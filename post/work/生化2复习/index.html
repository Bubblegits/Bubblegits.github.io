Chapter 13 Bioenergetics and biochemical reaction types#

Recall a basic formula that defines free energy G in chemistry: $$ ΔG = ΔH-TΔS $$ where $H$ stands for enthalpy and $S$ stands for entropy. As for biological reactions, they happen in a relatively fixed range of temperature, and $ΔS$ is determined by reactants and products, so $ΔG$ value changes according to ΔH (which is often determined by the amount of ATP in the reaction).

catabolism represents the reactions that release energy, and anabolism includes reactions that needs energy.

I’m not quite sure whether Schrödinge is the first one to raise the idea that the stuff of life is related to enthalpy. Anyway, this idea do seems amazing, but we will leave the amazing things for the physicists and go on with boring things.

Our chemistry lesson didn’t finish here. Remember that $$ ΔG=ΔG^{‘o}+RTlnQ\ ΔG^{‘o}=-RTlnK $$ Mind that $ΔG^{‘o}$ used in biology is measured under pH=7 environment. Since the reaction is affected by concentration of reactants and products, the $ΔG$ in real situation should be calculated as above. Approximately speaking, ΔG^{‘o}=-30.5kJ/mol can tell us whether a reaction releases free energy or receives free energy, but this is not accurate because the concentration is far from $1mol/L$.

We take a look at the famous reaction: $$ ATP+H_2O\rightarrow ADP+Pi,ΔG^{‘o}=-30.5kJ/mol\ 1cal=4.184J $$ This is an exergonic reaction (this fancy word means that this reaction releases energy).

Though ATP act as source or storage of energy in most reactions, certain muscle tissue takes a different way. They store energy through phosphocreatine.

Phosphocreatine act as a stable Pi carrier. Through this reaction, the cells control its ATP level in moderate concentration. A resting human consumes about 40 kg of ATP in 24 hours. Another interesting fact is that luciferase, an enzyme that produces fluorescent in fireflies consumes little ATP so they can measure ATP in a few picomoles (10^-12mol)

Now we will talk about oxidation and reduction. Chemistry tell us that $$ ΔE=E_o-E_r\ E=E^o+\frac{RT}{nF}ln\frac{[acceptor]}{[donor]} $$ and the connection between $ΔG$ and $ΔE$ is shown in this equation $$ ΔG=-nFΔE $$ Some of the energy released in reactions is directly stored in ATP, but other reaction (especially those that generate NADH, NADPH, and FADH_2) will create electrochemical potential and indirectly leads to ATP synthesis. Note that the vitamin form of NAD and NADP is niacin.

Chapter 16 Citric acid cycle#

This is the hall of fame in metabolism because the intermediates in this cycle will appear many times in other metabolic pathways. CAC is also called tricarboxylic acid cycle (TCA) or Krebs cycle (named after the one who discovered it), occurs in eight steps in mitochondria.

The whole cycle can be divided into three stages. In stage I, all the fuel molecules (including pyruvate) are turned into acetyl-CoA. In the second stage, acetyl-CoA is completely oxidized. And in the last stage, oxidation phosphorylation happens.

We may intentionally bind TCA to glycolysis, but in fact TCA can be the downstream of many pathways.

Yet here we mainlly focus on how Acetyl-CoA is oxidized from pyruvate. This reaction is catalyzed by an enzyme called PDH, with the help of TPP.

The long lipoyllysine(硫辛酰赖氨酸) chain is responsible for the switch between different catalytic subunits.

So now we have acetyl-CoA, and we can start the cycle. The whole cycle looks like this.

I don’t have much to say about this cycle. Just note the steps that synthesize GTP(ATP), NADH and FADH_2.

The complete oxidation of glucose generates 30 or 32 ATP, depending on the way of NADPH transfer.

Remember that TCA is the hall of fame, the members in it has their own shining points.

This raises a question: where does the intermediates come from? A main source is from pyruvate or phosphoenolpyruvate.

Pyruvate needs biotin as a coenzyme.

According to the usual procedure, now we should start talking about the regulation of TCA. We just take a look at the irreversible steps. They follow the general rule of negative feedback: inhibited by products and stimulated by ingredients.

Ca^2+ serves as the stimulation signal in muscle, so many catabolic reactions will be stimulated by Ca^2+.

Another cycle called glyoxylate cycle(乙醛酸循环), not present in vertebrates, can convert acetate into carbonhydrade. It uses two acetyl-CoA to synthesize a succinate.

The difference between this cycle and TCA lies in isocitrate lyase and malate synthase.

Chapter 17 Fatty Acid Catabolism#

Fatty acid is highly reduced and hydrophobic, so it releases much energy in oxidation and don’t need water in storage. Besides, it is quite stable and must be activated to join a chemical reaction.

We take in lots of triacylglycerol everyday. For dietary triacylglycerol, it will first be emulsified by bile salt (胆盐), then go through lipase digestion to be absorbed to intestinal mucosa (肠粘膜), reconverted into triacylglycerol and packed in chylomicrons.

Triacylglycerol is digested again in capillary (毛细血管), then stored in myocyte or adipocyte.

As you can imagine, to distribute the stored triacylglycerol in adipocyte, hormone signals are needed. The hormones needed here are mainly epinephrine and glucagon which release the fatty acid to the target tissue. Glycerol can be converted to D-Glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate and join glycolysis.

The important thing here is the degradation of fatty acid, a long chain molecule. The earliest researches on this uses synthetic label, which is a technology before radioisotopes.

Fatty acids are first oxidized on the outer membrane of mitochondria.

The activation step will produce fatty acyl-CoA and this step requires ATP. In fact acyl-CoA is a common form of activated molecule, if we remember the acetyl-CoA in TCA.

The fatty acid-CoA is transported to the mitochondria through carnitine(肉毒碱) catalyzed by carnitine acyltransferase II. The transport process plays a role in regulation of fatty acid catabolism.

Palmitoyl is a fully saturated fatty acid, but remember that we also have unsaturated fatty acid. If we take a look at the β-oxidation process, we will find out that double bond will be generated, but in trans configuration. So we need isomerase to turn the cis conformation in unsaturated fatty acid into trans conformation.

As for polyunsaturated fatty acid, we need both isomerase and reductase.

For odd number fatty acid, when it go through β-oxidation, a three-carbon propionyl-CoA will be synthesized in the last step. The propionyl-CoA (in help of coenzyme B12) will be converted into succinyl-CoA and then join the TCA.

As we have already talked about before, the rate of β-oxidation is regulated by the entering rate of fatty acyl-CoA. An important regulator of this process is malonyl-CoA(丙二酸辅酶A).

Plants have mitochondria(I thought that they don’t have mitochondria until i just googled it), but fatty acid oxidation mainly happens in peroxisomes(过氧化物酶体). The energy, however, will be released as heat, not ATP. And the acetyl-CoA generated will go through glyoxylate cycle, gluconeogenesis and be turned into glucose. Another interesting fact about β-oxidation in peroxisomes is that the whole process is catalyzed by one big complex.

Next we will talk about ketone bodies. These chemicals are synthesized by acetyl-CoA in liver.

The reverse reaction of this can happen extrahepatically, so we can consider ketone bodies as a transport form of acetyl-CoA.

Chapter 18 Amino acid oxidation and the production of urea#

This catabolism pathway mainly happens in animals. The amino acids can be fully oxidized or converted to other storable fuels.

First we take a look at the fate of dietary proteins. They are digested by enzymes like pepsin, trypsin, chymotrypsin and carboxypeptidase into small peptide chains.

The special thing about amino acid is that it has amino group. The amino group will be reused or excreted as ammonia (NH^4+), urea (CO(NH2)2) or uric acid. Carbon skeleton will be degraded through similar steps as fatty acid.

For vertebrates, liver is the major site of amino acid degradation. Amino groups are carried to liver mitochondria through Gln, Glu and Ala (from muscle) for further processing.

This figure shows how amino acids release their amino group. Ala (in help of α-ketoglutarate) and glutamine is turned into glutamate . Then glutamate takes off its amino group and turn back to α-ketoglutarate.

PHP facilitate the transamination reactions. These reactions transfer the amino groups in amino acids to α-ketoglutarate, synthesizing glutamate. Glutamate collects free ammonia, turn to glutamine and goes to liver.

For human beings, the NH4+ in liver is converted into urea

This is another important cycle in metabolism, linked with TCA.

The urea cycle is controlled through the allosteric regulation. N-acetylglutamate stimulates carbamoyl phosphate synthetase I. There is also gene-level regulation. Genetic defects of the urea production can lead to hyperammonemia (高血氨症) and brain damage. These diseases can be treated through ingesting certain chemicals like benzoate.

The carbon skeletons of amino acid can be turned into different metabolism intermediates (based on their unique structure).

Chapter 19 Oxidation phosphorylation and photophosphorylation#

In oxidative phosphorylation, electron flows from NADH or FADH_2 to O2. On the other hand, in photophosphorylation, electron flows from H2O to NADPH.

Though the flow of electrons seem different, they actually have similar mechanism. They create a proton gradient and synthesize ATP with it.

oxidation phosphorylation#

Oxidation phosphorylation happens in mitochondria. This guy looks like this:

Pay special attention to what the inner membrane contains, for this is where the oxidation phosphorylation happens. This inner membrane are folded into cristae and have large area.

As we have talked about before, the electrons from NADH and FADH2 will be released and transported to O2. In the whole process, four (really complicated) complex are involved and Coenzyme Q (also called Ubiquinone) often act as a mobile carrier of electron or proton. Some prosthetic (tool-like) groups are hemed and iron-sulfur clusters. The Fe here can act as a one-electron carrier.

How do we know the order of the complexes in the respiratory chain? A basic principle is that electrons transfer from low E to high E, we take a look at this.

We can also study this problem through specific inhibitors. Actually these inhibitors are still used in modern days. That means they may occur in the final exam.

So now we can start talking about the complexes. The first one is called NADH dehydrogenase. We can tell from the name that it receives electrons from NADH. It pumps four protons consuming one molecule of NADH. This guy looks like this.

Clever you will ask now: “How does FADH2 enter the respiratory chain?” They enter through succinate dehydrogenase (remember that turning succinate into fumarate is the only step that synthesize FADH2), and this enzyme doesn’t pump porton.

We see heme here.

These two enzymes all give their electrons to coenzyme Q, and the story of electrons goes on.

QH2 now gives its electrons to cytochrome c through cytochrome bc1 complex, also called complex III. This guy consists of three subunits: cytochrome b, an Fe-S protein and cytochrome c1.

Note that Q is and two-electron carrier, and cyt c is an one-electron carrier. The mechanism is quite interesting, detail can be found in figure 19-12 of the textbook.

Finally, the electrons of Cyt c are transferred to O2 on cytochrome oxidase (complex IV).

Four electrons are needed to oxidize a molecule of O2 to H2O, and 4H+ is pumped with it.

Now we can take a thorough look at the respiratory chain.

Each molecule of NADH will cause the chain to pump out 10 H+, for FADH2, the number is 6.

In early times, scientists have found that the electron transfer is coupled with ATP synthesize. Neither reaction occurs without the other. To explain this phenomenon, chemiosmotic model was proposed. This model introduced proton moving force.

Some chemicals such as DNP and FCCP are able to dissipate the proton gradient, thus uncoupling electron tansfer and phosphorylation.

In this figure, venturicidin and oligomycin inhibits the activity of ATP synthase. We can see the coupling and uncoupling relationship clearly.

Now lets focus on the ATP synthase. The F1 factor is the one discovered first, an amazing fact about it is that the ΔG of ATP synthesis on it is almost zero (a reversible reaction), and the energy produced from H+ gradient is used to release the ATP from the enzyme.

The F1 part consists of nine subunits of five types: 3α3βγδε. Aside from F1, ATP synthase also have a proton channel called Fo (not F zero), which have more than ten subunits in three types: ab2c10-12

N side is the matrix side and P side is the intermembrane space.

Under the motivation of H+, Fo starts to rotate, causing conformational change in β subunits. each of them. take turns to take up ADP + Pi, synthesize ATP, and release ATP. It’s like three workers work in different times.

Generally speaking, 4 proton will motivate a 120 degree turn, synthesizing a molecule of ATP.

The H+ gradient have uses other than ATP synthesis. It is also used in transport of ATP or ADP+Pi, the rotary motion of the bacterial flagella and producing heat(especially in brown fat tissue).

Glycolysis also generate NADPH, these NADPH in cytosol needs a way to enter the mitochondria. malate(苹果酸)-aspartate shuttle is often used.

In some conditions (such as skeletal muscle and brain), the NADPH goes through Glycerol-3-phosphate shuttle, turning to FADH2. This will cause energy loss.

For plants, they have external NADPH dehydrogenase (complex I), so they don’t need to concern about NADPH transport.

The whole ATP synthesis pathway, from glycolysis to TCA to respiratory chain, is tightly regulated.

photophosphorylation#

Photophosphorylation happens in chloroplast and includes two processes, light-dependent reactions and light-independent reactions.

First we will introduce chloroplast. This is an organelle, containing many thylakoids (means “like a pouch”) which gives it large membrane space. Thylakoids pile up to form grana, and the space between grana is called stroma.

The light-dependent reaction happens right on the stroma. The first step of light-dependent reaction is to absorb light. Common light absorption pigments are chlorophyll a, b (in plants) and bacteriochlorophyII (in bacteria). The former functions with Mg2+

These pigments come together with other proteins to form light-harvesting complexes (LHCs). Carotenoids and lutein are an important type of accessory pigment which makes the absorption range wider.

Chapter 25 DNA metabolism#

The deduced double helix structure indicated indicated that DNA has a semiconservative mechanism of replication. This is proved by a famous experiment conducted in E. coli cells. After labeling the DNA with $^{15}N$ which makes the chain heavier, they found that the newly synthesized DNA has H-H, H-L and L-L. Yet this is a rough understanding on DNA replication. Some details remains to be determined.

Deep going study is carried out with autoadiography. As for E. coli whose chromosomal DNA is a huge circle, daughter strands are synthesized immediately after parental strands separate. No temporary creation of linear DNA occurred during replication of the circular DNA. DNA replication begin at specific sites and proceed bidirectionally. After this, the researchers purified the DNA polymerase and studied DNA polymerization in vitro.

Basic information is that when DNA polymerase works in vitro, it needs DNA template, primer and dNTPs. The fundamental reaction for DNA synthesis is a nucleophilic attack by the 3’-OH group of the strand on the 5’ α-phosphorus of a incoming dNTP selected via base-pairing.

DNases can be divided into exonuclease and endonuclease. The former remove the nucleotide one by one, either from 5’ to 3’ or from 3’ to 5’, while the latter can cut the DNA at any site. DNA pol I can be divided into two fragments.

In DNA pol I, 3’->5’ exonuclease activity is able to remove mismatched base pairs, in order to proofread the nucleotides, and 5’ -> 3’ enables it to catalyze the nick translation process which removes RNA primers and can be used in DNA repair.

The synthesis of daughter DNA strands is semidiscontinuous. One of the strands (called leading strand) is synthesized continuously and the other one (called the lagging strand) discontinuously. Okazaki fragments occurs on the lagging strand. These Okazaki fragments will be join together by DNA lingase to form a complete strand.

Despite all these discussions about DNA pol I, this enzyme is found not to be responsible for DNA replication. Its velocity is too low and it can only add about 50 nucleotide before it dissociates from the template. The one that is responsible for DNA replication is actually DNA pol III which have no 5’ ->3’ exonuclease activity.

DNA pol III has three main subunits. α subunit has polymerization activity, ε subunit has 3’ -> 5’ proofreading exonuclease activity, and β subunit has clamp activity that binds the whole enzyme tightly to the DNA.

Aside from DNA polymerase III, many other proteins are also part of the replisome for DNA replication in E. coli, such as

  1. the helicase (dnaB) that consumes ATP to separate the two strands.
  2. the topoisomerases that relieve the topological strains in the helical structure.
  3. the single strand binding protein (SSB) that binds to the single-stranded DNA generated.
  4. the primase (dnaG) that generates a short RNA primer.
  5. the DNA polymerase I (polA) that removes the primer and replace it by a DNA sequence.
  6. the DNA ligase for sealing nicks between the Okazaki fragments.

In E. coli, the replication starts from a 245bp fragment called OriC. OriC contains 11 GATC sequences, the A can be methylated, replication is conducted once a time.

The whole process of E. coli chromosomal DNA replication has three stages: initiation, elongation and termination. Various enzymes are needed in initiation, and the synthesis of leading and lagging strand is believed to be conducted by a DNA pol III dimer. During termination stage, the two replication forks (moving in opposite direction) meet at the Ter sequence, and DNA topoisomerase IV separated the newly generated chromosome with the old one.

Chapter 26 RNA metabolism#

There are different types of RNA, some important types are rRNA (ribosome RNA), tRNA (transfer RNA) and mRNA (messanger RNA). RNA can also act as enzymes, there is a hypothesis that RNA catalyzes most of the metabolic reactions before protein occurred.

Three types of RNA polymerase catalyze the transcription of most RNA.

Type Localization Transcript Effect towards α-amanitin
I nucleolus 18S, 5.8S, 28S rRNA insecsitive
II nucleopalsm mRNA, snRNA strongly sensitive
III nucleopalsm tRNA, 5S rRNA inhibited by high concentration

The mRNA that just come through transcription will be further processed before it goes into translation. The further process include 5’ end capping, removing introns (this is discovered by an interesting EM experiment), linking exons and 3’ end polyadenylating. tRNA will also be cleaved, spliced and modified.

There are four types of introns that are spliced through different mechanisms. A single mRNA can be spliced differently to encode different protein.

A single pre-rRNA can generate different types of mature RNA in prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells.
