Introduction#

Main point: Freud’s theory is about techniques used to access and impact the unconscious. Students nowadays often focus on the obstacle of reaching the repressed (resistance, defense and transference) rather than the repressed itself, however, Lacan suggests that these obstacles, especially transference, should be avoided rather than confronted.

Chapter1 Tracing a symptom back to its origin#

Main point: It’s important for Freud to study the first appearance of a symptom in detail, because this helps find its origin (“precipitating cause”). Symptoms form when a thought is obliged and the event giving rise to the thought is therefore forgotten (the whole process is called repression), so the analysand is required to go back to the scene and say what he/she feels.

The ubiquity of isolation strategies#

Chapter3#

How to approach a dream#

After the analysand talks about his/her dream, analysts should break down the text into signifiers. They then return the isolated signifiers to analysands to find other signifiers associated to it. This can be hard because we often understand other people’s word as the signified but forget the second level——the signifier. Analysts should also pay attention to the hesitations and vacillations in dream text because they indicate a resistance.

How to get someone to associate to a dream#

An analysand tend to have his/her own logic when making associations. A figure in the dream is often composite, showing characteristics of several people in real life (condensation). The person in the dream can also be associated with some one who have a similar name (displacement).

Why are the wishes in dreams so often counterintuitive?#

Main Points: Dreams are compromise between unconscious wishes and semiwaking conscious. There is a censorship (the bar between conscious and unconscious) that help unconscious wishes go pass conscious, constituting a third party.

Author | Censor | Public Unconscious | Preconscious | Conscious

Subjectivity includes all three layers, namely:

  1. the urges
  2. the part that knows nothing about the urges
  3. the part that censure the urges

We often identify with others and take their urges as our own (Man’s desire is the other’s desire – Lacan), this can make us feel the desire to be foreign. This is especially important in self-destructing acts which actually makes someone around us happy. Some dreams can also be designed to thwart other’s wishes.

An unconscious wish is formulated in a complete sentence (or, the unconscious is structured like a language)#

Main point: A wish (conscious or unconscious) can often be stated (or formulated) into a sentence that takes the form of “I hope …” or “If only …”. This formulation requires synthetic effort. (What’s the role of the sentence**?**) In other words, the unconscious also thinks.

How not to approach a dream#

Main point: We should avoid “symbolic method” (make up stories that predict the future) and “decoding method” (give signifiers a fixed meaning) when interpreting dreams. A signifier in a dream means whatever the dreamer associates to it. From this point of view, some of Freud’s writings that supports a fixed sexual interpretation (such as phallus) are questionable.

Overdetermination: A simple example#

Main points: dream often have multiple meanings.

Tools for interpretation#

Main points: There are generally four ways of interpretating a dream-element:

  1. Take it in a positive or negative sense
  2. Interpretate it historically
  3. Interpretate it symbolically
  4. Interpretate on its wording

This part also mentioned some practical technique of interpretating a dream.

Does absolutely every dream fulfill a wish?#

Main points: Some of Freud’s early opinions, such as “every dream fulfill a wish”, seems to be unfalsifiable. He changed his opinion in late works that propose “trauma”, where dream end up generating anxiety. Trauma may be a distinct human aberration which has something to do with language.

Daydreams, Fantasies, Slips, and bungled actions (parapraxes).#

Main points: Daydreams and fantasies are similar in structure to dreams.

Dreams and memories#

Main points: Freud’s initial work holds the opinion that all neurosis results from sexual trauma (obsession or hysteria) caused by “seduction”. Later, he thinks that the whole question of what really happened is less important than the effect of the (often fantasized) event on the analysand. Dreams don’t necessarily represent recovered memories.

Chapter 4 Obsession and the case of the Rat Man#

Main points: Many people find something helpful in the case of the Rat Man. He shows indecisiveness, uncertainty and doubt that indicates obsession. What’s more, he has a belief that he is superior to everyone as well as a fear that he is worthless.

The “precipitating cause” of his illness as an adult#

Main points: the rat man’s illness is caused by a repetition of the marital situation of his father: to choose from “a poor and deserving woman” and a young, beautiful woman.

The paralyzing effect of “destiny”#

Main points: We often find analysands to be in the similar situation with their parents.

What was he doing instead of working?#

Main points: The rat man was masturbating as a form of rebellion. He also wished his parents suffer, but this wish is punished by the conscious through praying.

The structure of obsessive symptoms#

Main points: Obsession has a looping structure. In the rat man’s case, his sexual urge (force 1) is (indirectly) prohibited by his father(force 2), so he developed a wish to kill his father (back to force 1) and, further more, a fear of this wish being fulfilled (force 2 again) or wising to undo things that fulfilled the wish. The obsessive is only conscious of the fear, which ultimately repress the urge, other steps are ellipsed.

In order to maintain the balance between force one and force two, the obsessive tend to not making choices. By putting cathexis(energy) into the conflict, he/she may feel a sexual joy.

Jouissance in obsession#

Jouissance can be taken as satisfaction of an unbearably aggressive drive. In rat man’s case, punishment from his father gives him jouissance, so does imagining Gisa or his father being tortured.

The rat man’s childhood neurosis#

The rat man can’t remember his traumatic childhood experience at the beginning, in this case, some hypothesis should be proposed by the analyst.

Transference and Transferences#

The rat man sometimes view Freud as the captain or his father, both of them had done something cruel to him. This behavior of transference help him act out things that he didn’t realize.

Postmortem#

Main points: The rat man didn’t completely finish his analysis, maybe because Freud was pushing him too hard. Yet the analysis, especially the transference that tells him more about his rage, helped a lot.

Some forms taken by obsession today: doing nothing and “nothing doing”#

Main points: Regarding the conflict in obsessives mentioned above, people who diligently doing nothing can be considered obsessive. Freud’s famous theory of oral, anal and genital stages also come with conflicts (between parents and children), the conflicts deprive us of jouissance and leads to our rebellion that gives us pleasure, rebellion by doing nothing (as our parents require).

Going through the motions: doing something as if one did not want to#

Some obsessives (especially perfectionists) “do and undo then redo a great many things.” They feel they are doing this for the significant other, so they leave some time “just for themselves”, often during midnight. There are also many other curious forms of obsessiveness.

Nothing else matters#

The protest in obsession seems to indicate that nothing compensates for their early loss. This is pretty much true for all of us, but obsessive have an especially strong feeling that it’s too late for them to do anything, demanding for a “replacement life”. CBT(cognitive-behavioral therapy) focus on the present and neglect such deprivation.

Chapter 5 Hysteria and the case of Dora#

A lot of evidences show that Freud took control throughout Dora’s analysis and gave her little freedom. The main reason for this is that Freud wants to show his psychotherapeutic method to the world through this case.

Synopsis of Ida’s situation#

Ida’s father had had syphilis prior to his marriage. He also had an affair with Frau K, an unhappy wife who was on the edge of divorce, after Ida’s whole family moved to a new town. Ida’s mother is a housewife and was unhappy about the marriage.

Ida’s “Entry” into analysis#

Though Ida herself didn’t want to change anything, her father insisted that she should go see Freud. Part of the reason that Freud “overly invested in the treatment” is that he want Ida to be impressed by his insights so that the analysis goes on.

The unfolding of Ida’s analysis#

The situation looks like a quartet: Ida was given to Herr K in trade of Frau K. Ida’s main concern is that she can do nothing to affect the relationship with her father and Frau K. However, some facts of Ida aided the relationship appeared during the analysis. Reproaches directed to others are often self-reproaches. The conscious notion “others are to blame” hides the unconscious notion “I am to blame”. This is the “dialectical reversal”.

The why and wherefore of Ida’s accusation#

The incident that shook up the quartet might be Herr K’s proposition. Herr’s remark on Frau being worthless shook Frau’s position (不知道這是不是一個可行的表達) in Ida’s structure of desire as an idol, and brought the romantic love to a sexual level. Ida may also identified with a governess to whom Herr K had propositioned in almost the same way. Yet all these explanations didn’t get conformation from Ida.

Further Mysteries#

Something curious is that Ida wasn’t angry at Frau K, the one who is involved in her father’s affair and revealed Ida’s secrets to Herr K. Freud initially believed that Ida was jealous of Frau K because she took her father away from her, but the mystery seems to indicate that Ida wanted Frau K’s love for herself. The jealousy (What jealousy**?** The one that Freud assumed Ida had of Frau K**?** Or the one of Ida’s father**?** What Fink wrote here is “her apparent jealousy of the relationship”) hides the desire for Frau K, that’s the “second dialectical reversal”. Yet Freud didn’t quite realize Ida’s love for Frau K’s, maybe because of the unanalyzed homosexual current in himself. If we analyze Ida’s love for Frau, we may find that Frau K was actually a mystery of Ida’s own femininity (according to Lacan). In other words, she wanted to be desired as Frau K was.

Femininity#

Every man appears to know what masculinity means, it means basically everything associated with power (or phallus). But femininity seems to be a mystery for woman because it ranges from being virginal to being outrageous. According to Lacan, phallus is the signifier for man, yet there is no signifier for woman. From Ida’s perspective, Frau K is an idol from whom she wants to learn femininity. Anyway, the case of Dora is complicated and Freud, maybe Lacan as well, had fallen in the trap that they thought they knew all.

Transference revisited and the breaking off of the analysis#

Transference is a quite confusing concept. A common definition of transference is the phenomenon that the analysand’s feeling about someone is transferred onto the analyst. The transference (largely aroused by Freud himself) of Ida’s hatred towards Herr K onto Freud may caused the termination of Ida’s analysis.

Freud’s definition of Transference#

Freud gave definition of transference as “not created but brought to light by psychoanalysis”, “simply reprints of the same same story”, “not only onto the doctor but also onto all the other aspects of the current situation.” Note that “the current situation” not only includes feelings.

How can transference be dealt with?#

Transferences may not disappear even when we have successfully revealed them. We often need to track them back to their source. To avoid giving rise to transference, the analyst show act less dominantly.

What might have happened#

Ida’s hostility towards Freud are caused not only by transference, but also by the violence Freud himself inflicted her. Ida was unwilling to give up the jouissance derived from the symptoms, yet Freud didn’t express objection to her decision of quitting the analysis, maybe because of a resistance in himself (did Freud fall in love with Ida?). Freud said that he should have realized Ida’s love for Frau K, yet what should be seriously reflected is his technique.

Countertransference#

Countertransference is the transference of analyst upon analysand. Freud put Ida in the great ambition of establishing his theory of hysteria, or we may assume that Freud himself identified with Herr K. Or we can ask this question: why did Freud write up this incomplete case at all? In memory of Ida?

Relevance and conclusion#

We should frankly talk with others to deal with our own symptoms. Besides, the lion’s share of interpreting the story should be left to the analysand (Was Freud left the chance to free associate with his symptoms that emerged during the analysis with Ida**?**) In Ida’s case, Freud had two inappropriate identities, a parent and a suitor.

Chapter 6 Symptom Formation#

Both symptoms and elementary phenomena (hallucinations, delusions) are produced by negating mechanism, yet the former is produced by repression and the latter is produced by foreclosure. Contemporary psychotherapy have made up many disorders, but psychoanalysis only have a few clinical structures, and the process of analysis focuses on the symptoms that the patient complains of. Many analysands spontaneously view more aspects of their life as symptoms as analysis proceeds.

The structural viewpoint#

Symptoms can be found in everyone. Everyone has repressed some wishes that generate pleasure, and symptoms are the endeavor to get the pleasure back. Some people don’t complain about their symptoms because they have found acceptable roundabouts to the pleasure.

How symptoms form#

As mentioned before, symptoms form owing to a conflict between force 1 and force 2. Following examples give vivid picture on symptoms

The “Sense” of Symptoms#

Each detail of a symptom can be meaningful. The “sense” of symptoms include two things: the experience from which the symptom arouses and the intention the symptom serves. The intention of a symptom can be to escape from a duty or to send a message to someone.

Symptoms involve wishes#

Symptoms presents us with the realization of a wish in the present tense through a fight with counterwish.

Symptoms involve repetition, but with a twist#

Main points: Symptoms are sometimes ritualized actions that represents a early scene. This repetition may bring some kind of satisfaction by leaving a desire unfulfilled (it can be terrible if the desire is actually fulfilled).

Note: “Twist” here seems to have some connection with metonymy.

A symptom is not equivalent to a structure#

In psychotherapeutic diagnosis, we should rely on not only the symptom, but also the intention of the symptom.

Symptom bring jouissance with them#

Symptoms provides us with a form of satisfaction through the experience of suffering, Lacan called this satisfaction “jouissance”. Jouissance can be a substitute for sexual satisfaction, for example, panic attack looks like sexual excitation. Yet it is doubtful whether all symptoms bring satisfaction that substitutes sexual satisfaction, jouissance can at least substitute for satisfaction of aggression.

Symptoms are overdetermined#

A symptom may have multiple causes and multiple intentions.

Symptoms signal the return of the repressed#

Symptoms form to escape form anxiety, yet repressed anxiety often returns little by little.

The physical cause of symptoms#

Language plays an important role in physical symptoms such as headache. Many evidence also suggest that both the mind and the body can be treated by talking to someone.

On other so-called neuroses#

Freud distinguished between “psychoneurosis” and “actual neurosis”. “Actual neurosis” refers to the disorders whose cause isn’t psychological, but some kind of physical state. The word “actual” here means something like “current”. Freud also introduced “narcissistic neuroses” and “transference neurosis”.
